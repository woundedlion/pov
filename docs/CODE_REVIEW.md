# Holosphere — Code Quality Review

*Second independent multi-agent audit, 2026-06-09. Eleven reviewers each read one component end to end and cross-referenced the 1,900-line README to recover the intended contract; this report synthesizes their findings, **adversarially verifies every P0/P1 claim against the source**, and forms its own appraisal. Out of scope by request: `core/effects_legacy.h`, `targets/Holosphere/Holosphere.ino`, and `core/rotate.h`; the third-party `core/FastNoiseLite.h` is treated as a vendored dependency. Letter grades (A+ … F) are measured against a high professional / graphics-middleware bar — not "does it run." The comparative appraisal in §7 is derived from first principles and deliberately does not reuse the prior audit's framing, wording, or conclusions.*

*This pass post-dates the prior audit's P0–P3 remediation. The previously **rejected** findings — StereoShaderBase dedup, live-param-sync factoring, the reaction-graph `PROGMEM` placement, the Liquid2D `Driver` wrap, and the "double-buffer tearing" non-issue — were not re-raised, and are not re-litigated here.*

## 1. Executive Summary

Holosphere is a single C++20 rendering engine (~50k LOC across `core/`, `effects/`, `hardware/`, `targets/`, plus the sibling `daydream/` JavaScript simulator) that compiles to two Teensy 4.x persistence-of-vision LED-sphere firmware targets — a 96×20 single-controller *Holosphere* and a four-microcontroller 288×144 *Phantasm* — and to a WebAssembly module that drives a Three.js browser simulator. The same effect classes, the same 335 KB arena, and the same per-frame `Pixel16[]` buffer run unchanged across all three targets; resolution is a compile-time `<W,H>` template parameter, so the device build carries no overhead from generality.

The dominant finding, again, is **coherence of judgment**. Eleven reviewers handed disjoint slices returned the same description of the author's values: a fail-fast `HS_CHECK` doctrine placed deliberately on *cold* seams and explicitly kept off the per-pixel hot loop (where a stripped `assert`, documented as a deliberate choice, is used instead); comments that encode *contracts and rationale* rather than restating code; a 16-bit-linear / OKLCH perceptual color pipeline and a compile-time `Pipeline<W,H,Filters…>` filter chain that multiple reviewers independently named the best abstractions they saw. The safety story remains **mechanically verified** — a re-exec death harness that asserts the *exact* illegal-instruction status for 24 cold-path invariants, a determinism harness requiring byte-identical re-renders under a mock clock and pinned RNG, host-testable hardware protocol and segment-tiling layers, and a WASM zero-copy memory-view lifetime that is correctly engineered on both sides of the boundary.

What is notable about *this* pass is how little remains. The prior audit's substantive defects have been remediated, and the raw machine-generated finding set this time was dominated by **false positives and by-design decisions** that did not survive verification. Four of the most severe candidate findings were dismissed on inspection:

- a claimed P0 "inverted epsilon" between `Vector::normalize()` and `normalized_or()` — they use the **same** `epsilon` threshold for opposite outcomes (one traps, one falls back), exactly as the in-code comment documents;
- a claimed P1 use-after-free in the WASM `getVertices()` accessor — `new Float32Array(typed_memory_view(...))` **copies synchronously** before the local vector is destroyed; the idiom is correct and the comment is accurate;
- a claimed P1 "incorrect" species blend in BZReactionDiffusion — it is deliberate sequential `over`-compositing of three concentrations, a legitimate aesthetic choice, not a math error;
- a claimed P1 fail-fast violation in `FunctionRef::operator()` — the stripped `assert` there is an explicitly documented deliberate hot-path decision, precisely the philosophy the codebase states.

After verification, **no P0 finding survives in scope** — the strongest remaining correctness item is a *defensive-parity* gap (a missing `HE_NONE` guard in one Hankin loop that its two siblings carry, reachable only on non-manifold input the registry never produces). The rest of the surviving debt is the ordinary residue of a fast solo project: a handful of internal-consistency gaps against the project's own patterns (a truncation that bypasses the file's own rounding helper; a bounds guard one effect carries and its twin doesn't; a degenerate-input fallback present in the `slerp` spline variant but missing from the `fast` one), a few real but bounded resource issues on the web side (segment workers not torn down on unload; a per-frame per-segment array copy), a couple of genuine test-coverage gaps (the `buffer_free()` spin-wait is never exercised; `Gradient::get` out-of-range behavior is documented-but-untested), and doc drift.

**Net: A− work, and a cleaner tree than the prior audit found.** A cohesive, philosophically consistent, exceptionally documented and verified engine whose remaining debt is small, mechanical, and entirely non-architectural.

## 3. Cross-Cutting Quality Dimensions

These grades are the whole-product synthesis across all components.

### Correctness & Robustness — **A−**
After verification, no memory-safety defect (use-after-free, OOB, alignment, arena overflow) was confirmed in the engine core, and the arena bounds math is provably wrap-safe with the proof in the comment. Numerical edges are guarded with uncommon consistency — stereographic projections, normalizes, and hot-path divisions carry documented epsilons or `normalized_or` fallbacks, and the NaN/Inf cluster is death-tested to *trap* rather than propagate. Held back from the A band only by a thin set of defensive-parity gaps where one site omits a guard its siblings carry: the Hankin `HE_NONE` walk (`hankin.h:213`), the IslamicStars topology index (`IslamicStars.h:93-94`), and `Spline::cubic_fast`'s missing degenerate-input fallback (`3dmath.h:1044`). None of these crash or mis-paint on the geometry the product actually renders; each is a robustness hardening, not a live bug. *(Dismissed this pass: a claimed `normalize()`/`normalized_or()` epsilon inversion — false; a `getVertices()` use-after-free — false; a BZ "incorrect blend" — a deliberate compositing choice; a `FunctionRef` fail-fast violation — a documented deliberate hot-path decision.)*

### Architecture & Design Elegance — **A**
The compile-time `Pipeline<W,H,Filters…>` with automatic world/screen/pixel domain bridging via discarded `if constexpr` branches remains a genuinely excellent abstraction — reviewers graded it against production filter/scene-graph systems and it holds up. The one-engine/three-targets seam leaks nothing into effect code; the owned-vs-borrowed `MeshState` duality, the purely-functional Conway operators over ping-ponged arenas, the CRTP `ReactionDiffusionBase`, the self-registering `REGISTER_EFFECT` factory with startup roster verification, and the POD `Feedback::Style` are all cohesive. The lone structural seam worth naming is that `SolidBuilder`'s fluent chain sidesteps the `generate()` arena-lifecycle wrapper the README presents as universal — a documentation/scope mismatch more than a design flaw.

### Interface Expressiveness — **A−**
`registerParam` → WASM → auto-generated GUI is a clean reflection seam; the `Fragment` shader interface with documented per-rasterizer register conventions, `SolidBuilder`'s fluent Conway chaining, `.then()` animation sequencing, and `ArenaSpan`'s type-level borrow-vs-own distinction are all expressive and hard to misuse by accident. Docked a half-step for ergonomic seams a new contributor *could* misuse: the `setClip(y0,y1,x0,x1)` Y-before-X argument order, the single-`onChange`-slot model in the GUI wrapper, and the two sibling shaders (Liquid2D/Flyby) that expose near-identical parameter sets but animate transitions differently.

### Performance & Resource Discipline — **A**
A standout. Compile-time resolution dispatch, `FunctionRef` zero-allocation type erasure, split-trig LUTs (~145× smaller than dense arrays), fast-reject heuristics, the fused three-species graph-Laplacian walk that triples nothing, branchless saturating color math, `fast_cbrt`/`fast_sinf` deployed *exactly* on the OKLab hot path and nowhere else, a single instanced draw call with GPU-side normalize, and worker parallelism measured as max() not sum(). Memory is rigorous to the byte. The only waste found this pass is on the web side: a per-frame per-segment `new Uint16Array(msg.pixels)` recopy that defeats the structured-clone transfer (`segment_controller.js:166`).

### Documentation & Comments — **A+**
Again the highest-scoring dimension. Comments encode *why*, not *what* — the `lerp16` signed-`smlad` unsoundness, the no-wrap arena-bounds derivation, the relaxed-atomics single-observer proof, the equal-priority-ISR LOAD-BEARING invariant, the `-fno-finite-math-only` ordering, and numerous "do NOT change this" guards that record past bugs. The 1,900-line README is genuine architectural documentation. The only defects are *drift*: the `generators.h`/`SolidBuilder` scope claim, an undocumented `fast_atan2` singularity epsilon, a "Mirror of `narrow_index`" comment that over-claims equivalence, and a stale `§10.7` cross-reference in `wasm.cpp`.

### Testing & Verification — **A−**
A verified fail-fast death harness (re-exec, exact illegal-instruction status, 24 invariants), a determinism harness enforcing byte-identical re-renders under an injected clock, an auto-generated smoke pass that constructs and renders every effect at 288×144 with asserts on, hardware wire-format and segment-tiling proofs, and a WASM memory-view stability guard — fronted by three continuous layers (pre-commit hook, presubmit CI, gated deploy) that make a regression structurally unable to reach the live demo. The real gaps are narrow and now identifiable: the `buffer_free()` Canvas spin-wait — the one piece of logic whose inversion would hang only deployed hardware — is deliberately stepped around in tests and never exercised; `Gradient::get`'s out-of-`[0,1]` behavior is documented-but-untested; and the web side has only a single tooling sanity check.

### Consistency with Stated Philosophies — **A**
The design philosophies (16-bit linear color; compile-time resolution; fail-fast cold-only `HS_CHECK`; arena allocation; double-buffer-by-design) are applied with rare uniformity and *enforced at build/startup*, not merely documented. The deductions are exactly the places where the code deviates from its *own* rules: the `hue_rotate` truncation that bypasses `float_to_pixel16`, the two defensive guards present in one effect and absent in its twin, and the `generate()`-wrapper scope claim that `SolidBuilder` doesn't honor.

## 4. Prioritized Fix List

*Each item is independently verified against the source. Severity: P0 = mis-paints real hardware or a latent semantic trap; P1 = robustness / verification / resource risk; P2 = consistency & cosmetic correctness; P3 = doc drift & cleanup.*

**P0 — correctness on real hardware / latent semantic traps**

*None found in scope this pass. The prior audit's two hardware/feedback P0s have been remediated, and every P0/P1 candidate surfaced this round was either reclassified or dismissed on verification (see §4a). The strongest surviving correctness item is a defensive-parity gap, listed under P1.*

**P1 — robustness / verification / resource risks**

1. ✅ Add the missing `HS_CHECK(he_start.prev != HE_NONE)` (or the `?:` `pair` fallback) at `hankin.h:213` before `he_mesh.half_edges[he_start.prev].vertex`. Its two siblings in the same function (`hankin.h:130`, `hankin.h:175`) guard this exact access against the `HE_NONE` sentinel; this rosette-origin walk does not. Reachable only on a degenerate / non-manifold mesh — which the registered solids never produce — so it does not fire today, but the file's own pattern says it should be guarded. **Fixed:** added the `HS_CHECK(he_start.prev != HE_NONE)` guard with an explanatory comment matching the two sibling sites.
2. ✅ Terminate the segment-worker pool on page unload. `disposeApp()` (`daydream.js:640-654`) cleans up the sidebar and the Three.js side but never calls `segments.destroy()`, so enabling Segmented POV and navigating away leaks N live `Worker` instances (each holding a WASM module) until the tab closes. Mirror the existing explicit-dispose pattern. **Fixed:** `disposeApp()` now calls `segments.destroy()` (a no-op when the pool is empty), terminating every worker on a real page discard.
3. Add a test that actually exercises the `Canvas` constructor's `buffer_free()` spin-wait (`test_effects.h:98-101` currently steps around it via `advance_display()`). This is the one synchronization gate whose inversion would hang *only* on real hardware, where the ISR consumes frames asynchronously; a flag-or-timed test closes the gap without risking an actual hang.

**P2 — consistency & cosmetic correctness**

4. Route `hue_rotate`'s float→`uint16` conversion through `float_to_pixel16()` (`color.h:473-475`). It currently truncates (`static_cast<uint16_t>(std::clamp(nr*65535.0f, …))`), biasing every channel down by up to ~1/65535, while the file's own helper four lines later (`color.h:501-503`) rounds with `+0.5f` and the comment explicitly documents the anti-truncation rule. This is the same class as the already-fixed `BakedPalette::get` truncation; `hue_rotate` is a per-pixel hot path (default feedback transform + Flyby), so the bias is applied every frame. `frac` is already in range, so no extra clamp cost.
5. Add the topology-index bounds guard to IslamicStars' fragment shader (`IslamicStars.h:93-94`) to match its near-twin HankinSolids (`HankinSolids.h:100-103`), which guards `faceIdx >= 0 && faceIdx < topology.size()` before indexing and falls back to `0`. IslamicStars indexes `raw_indices[faceIdx]` directly. Defensive parity against a malformed face index.
6. Give `Spline::cubic_fast` the degenerate-input fallback its sibling already has (`3dmath.h:1044`). The Catmull-Rom cubic can sum to a near-zero vector for antipodal/coincident control points — legitimate geometry per the project's own philosophy — and the unconditional `normalized()` then traps. `cubic_slerp` avoids this via SLERPs; the fast path should use `normalized_or(result, p1)`.
7. Reconcile the two tolerances in `make_rotation` (`3dmath.h:906,915`): the unit-quaternion guard uses a 1% relative band while the near-zero cross-product axis test uses a `1e-4f` absolute threshold. They are incommensurate; pick one scale (relative, via squared magnitude) or document why they differ.
8. Avoid the per-frame per-segment array recopy in the worker composite path (`segment_controller.js:166`): `new Uint16Array(msg.pixels)` copies a buffer that was already transferred zero-copy. Use the transferred view directly (or a non-copying `new Uint16Array(buf, byteOffset, length)` view) so the structured-clone transfer isn't defeated.
9. Unify or document the param-lerp strategy divergence between Liquid2D (`Liquid2D.h:156-183`, staggered per-field) and Flyby (`Flyby.h:119-127`, parallel per-field). Two sibling stereographic shaders with near-identical parameter sets transition presets differently with no stated rationale. *(This is a behavioral-consistency note, distinct from the previously-rejected StereoShaderBase dedup — no shared base is proposed.)*
10. ✅ Return a `dispose()` handle from the tools' `initScene()` (`tools/shared.js:134-144`) that cancels the `requestAnimationFrame` loop and removes the `resize` listener, matching the cleanup discipline in `driver.js`/`daydream.js`. Tool pages are long-lived so impact is low, but the loop and listener currently run forever with no off switch. **Fixed:** `initScene` now tracks the rAF id and returns a `dispose()` that cancels the loop, removes the resize listener, and disposes the controls/renderer (daydream repo).

**P3 — doc drift & cleanup**

11. ✅ Document the `1e-10f` singularity epsilon added to `abs_y` in `fast_atan2` (`3dmath.h:252`); the surrounding comment covers only the Hastings accuracy, so the guard reads as an unexplained anomaly five orders of magnitude below the documented error. **Fixed:** added a comment explaining the guard keeps the (0,0) origin a finite ratio rather than a `0/0` NaN, well below the documented Hastings error.
12. ✅ Reconcile the `generate()`-wrapper scope claim: README §7.8 says *all* procedural geometry goes through `generate()`, but `SolidBuilder` (`solids.h:195+`) chains Conway operators directly without it. Either route `SolidBuilder` through the wrapper or narrow the README claim to direct `Solids::*` calls. **Fixed:** narrowed the README §7.8 claim to direct registry/effect creation and called out `SolidBuilder`'s self-managed two-arena ping-pong as a deliberate exception.
13. ✅ Fix the "Mirror of `narrow_index`" comment on `narrow_face_count` (`mesh.h:241`): the two guard different types and overflow ranges (`size_t` vs `INT16_MAX` for vertex indices; `int` vs `UINT8_MAX` for face side counts). They are parallel guards, not identical logic. **Fixed:** reworded to "parallel to `narrow_index` in intent" and spelled out the distinct input types and bounds.
14. Clarify the `Pixel::Feedback` scratch-arena comment (`filter.h:793-803`) to acknowledge that `Plot::rasterize` already takes a `scratch_arena_a` checkpoint in a disjoint phase of the same frame — the temporal-separation argument is correct but the comment omits the existing usage, leaving the safety case incomplete.
15. Tighten two hot-path comments so a future contributor can't regress them: the `Canvas::operator()` hot-loop `assert` (`canvas.h:439`) should state plainly "use `assert` here, not `HS_CHECK` — the one hot-loop exception," and the `buffer_free()` watchdog comment (`canvas.h:405-414`) should note the first `micros()` read happens inside the spin condition, not before it.
16. Fix the README cross-reference in `wasm.cpp:263`: the comment cites `§10.7` (Segmented POV Workers) but the surrounding context is the `§10.2` WASM memory-view contract.
17. Replace the `segment_id % segs_per_arm` modulo in `pov_segment_map.h:47` with `segment_id & (segs_per_arm - 1)` (`segs_per_arm` is validated power-of-two), matching the bitwise idiom already used in `read_id()`. Negligible at boot-time `constexpr`, purely stylistic consistency.

Close the small testing gaps the suite already flags in TODOs: add coverage for `Gradient::get` with `t` just outside `[0,1]` (or trap in `Gradient::get` per the fail-fast doctrine, since `GenerativePalette::lerp` can reach it), document the `fake_canvas()` "must not call `queue_frame()`" coupling in `test_animation.h`, and document the `hs::clamp` NaN-to-hi-bound contract that `test_blend_alpha_clamps_before_cast` silently relies on.

### 4a. Verified and dismissed this pass

*Recorded for the same reason the prior audit recorded its disproven findings — so they are not re-raised next time.*

- **`Vector::normalize()` vs `normalized_or()` "inverted epsilon" (claimed P0)** — *false.* Both use `std::numeric_limits<float>::epsilon()`. `normalize()` proceeds when `m >= epsilon` and traps otherwise; `normalized_or()` returns the fallback when `m < epsilon`. Same threshold, opposite outcomes, by design — the comment at `3dmath.h:215-218,234-238` documents exactly this split (trap on unexpected zero; graceful fallback at the handful of legitimate-degeneracy sites). No divergence.
- **WASM `getVertices()` use-after-free (claimed P1)** — *false.* `val::global("Float32Array").new_(val(typed_memory_view(n, data.data())))` constructs a JS `Float32Array` from a typed-array view, which **copies element-wise synchronously** during the `.new_()` call, before the local `std::vector data` is destroyed at function return. The comment "copying data" is accurate; this is the standard Emscripten idiom.
- **BZReactionDiffusion `blend_species` "incorrect formula" (claimed P1)** — *not a bug.* The chained `r = r*(1-x) + cx*x` form is deliberate `over`-compositing of the three species as layers, a legitimate visual choice; for the near-one-hot concentrations BZ actually produces it differs little from a weighted sum, and there is no correctness contract requiring the weights to sum to one. A rendering-aesthetic preference, not a defect.
- **`FunctionRef::operator()` fail-fast violation (claimed P1)** — *by design.* The `assert(thunk_ != nullptr)` at `concepts.h:75` is the documented hot-path choice ("Per-pixel hot path … deliberately NOT `HS_CHECK` to avoid an always-on per-call branch on-device"), which is precisely the stated philosophy: stripped `assert` on hot paths, cold trap at the bind site. Re-raising it would contradict an explicit, reasoned decision.
- **`targets/Holosphere/Holosphere.ino` findings (NUM_PIXELS, dead-code, stale comments)** — *out of scope* by the review rules; not assessed.

## 5. Overall Impression

Holosphere continues to read like the work of one strong engineer who internalized a small set of principles, applied them everywhere, and then *built the machinery to prove they hold*. The rare quality is not any single clever file — it is that eleven independent reviewers, each handed a different slice, came back with the same account of the author's values and the same shape of (now much smaller) weakness. Most codebases this size are an archaeology of several past selves; this one has a single, legible voice, and the voice has stayed consistent through a round of remediation.

The more telling result of *this* audit is what it took to find anything at all. The machine-generated finding set was disproportionately false positives and by-design decisions — a claimed epsilon inversion that was the same threshold read twice, a use-after-free that was a synchronous copy, a "wrong" blend that was a deliberate compositing choice, a fail-fast "violation" that was the fail-fast doctrine working as written. A codebase where the *aggressive* reading of the worst candidate findings collapses under a five-minute look at the source is a codebase whose real defect density is low. What remains after that filtering is uniformly minor: a guard present in one twin and missing in the other, a truncation that ignores the file's own rounding helper, a worker pool that isn't torn down on unload, a spin-wait that the tests politely avoid. None of it is rot; all of it is the ordinary debt of velocity, and the audit surfaced no architectural decision that needs reversing.

## 6. Technical & Artistic Merit — The Achievement

The achievement is best understood as **three hard problems solved at once on one codebase**:

1. **A real-time spherical rendering engine** with its own SDF scanline rasterizer, geodesic curve plotter, volumetric ray-marcher, half-edge mesh kernel carrying the full Conway operator algebra, a perceptual (OKLCH) 16-bit-linear color pipeline, and a compile-time-composed filter graph — all templated to zero overhead at a fixed resolution.

2. **Hard-real-time POV firmware** that paints a sphere by spinning an LED strip at 480 RPM, firing columns on a microsecond ISR budget, double-buffered lock-free, and — in the Phantasm build — *coordinated across four microcontrollers* over two sync wires with branchless per-segment ISRs and a deterministic cross-board RNG so all four paint an identical canvas.

3. **A browser digital twin** that compiles the identical C++ to WebAssembly, renders it through Three.js with zero-copy GPU-normalized pixel readback, and reproduces the four-board partitioning in generation-fenced Web Workers so the hardware layout can be exercised in software before fabrication.

The artistic layer is mathematically literate rather than decorative. The effects implement the *actual* Hopf fibration of S³ with a 4D tumble, the associated-Legendre recurrence for real spherical harmonics, Belousov-Zhabotinsky and Gray-Scott reaction-diffusion on a precomputed 7,680-node Fibonacci K-NN graph (with a fused three-species Laplacian walk), authentic Hankin-method Islamic star patterns generated procedurally from Conway-operated Archimedean solids, twisted-torus volumetrics with metallic Blinn-Phong and an analytic Lipschitz bound for safe sphere-tracing, and Möbius-warped grids with pole-stabilizing counter-rotation. These are implemented by someone who understands the geometry, not assembled from shader-toy fragments.

## 7. Comparative Appraisal — Independent Assessment

*Formed from first principles against each reference class. It does not draw on the prior audit's comparative section.*

**What class of artifact is this?** It is a *fabrication-grade rendering engine for a single exotic display geometry*, with a software twin maintained at bit parity. That combination — one renderer, simultaneously a microcontroller firmware, a multi-controller distributed system, and a WebAssembly module — is the lens to judge it through, because almost nothing else occupies all four roles at once.

**Against the maker / LED-art ecosystem.** The reference population here is FastLED/WLED sketches, addressable-LED installations, and one-off POV builds. The modal project in that world is a single-target Arduino sketch: 8-bit sRGB blending, tuned magic constants, no compile-time abstraction, no tests, no separation between effect and driver, and certainly no second implementation to check against. Holosphere is not a better instance of that template — it is a different kind of object. A perceptual linear-light pipeline, a templated zero-overhead engine, a verified fail-fast safety net, a CI-gated deploy, and a byte-faithful simulator are simply not features that ecosystem produces, and synchronized multi-controller POV is something it does not attempt at all. The distance is categorical, not incremental.

**Against professional creative-technology tooling.** The fair commercial comparison is the LED-mapping / generative-graphics stack — Pixelblaze at the embedded end, MadMapper and TouchDesigner and high-end architectural-LED controllers at the workstation end. Holosphere is far narrower (it renders one geometry, the sphere) but inside that scope its *practices* are the ones a serious graphics team would recognize: zero-overhead compile-time specialization, an explicit and documented memory model, host-testable hardware-protocol layers, build-provenance hashing, a reflection-driven GUI, and a determinism harness. Where it visibly trails a hardened product is in the dimensions a *team and a calendar* buy: there is no fuzzing, the glue layers carry the thinnest test coverage in the project, and a few internal-consistency gaps (a guard in one effect missing from its twin) are exactly what a mandatory second reviewer catches and a solo author doesn't. The core is product-quality; the seams still read as single-authored.

**Against academic / research work.** Taken individually, several of the techniques here would each be at home in a graphics-or-fabrication workshop paper or a strong conference demo: geodesic SDF rasterization on a sphere, graph reaction-diffusion on a Fibonacci lattice, procedural Hankin patterns over Conway operators, microsecond-budget multi-board POV synchronization. Where it plausibly *is* at the frontier is the specific conjunction it occupies — a perceptually-correct, compile-time-specialized, formally-fail-fast rendering engine that runs bit-identically across a four-microcontroller POV sphere and a WebAssembly browser twin, with a death harness proving its safety net fires. It would be surprising to find a more rigorous open instance of *that exact intersection*; the frontier it touches is one of integration and verification, not of any single primitive.

**Overall standing.** As solo work this is exceptional — and the second audit, which spent most of its effort *retiring* candidate findings rather than confirming them, raises rather than lowers that assessment.
