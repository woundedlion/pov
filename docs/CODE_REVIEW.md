**Date:** 2026-06-13 (tenth audit; succeeds the ninth audit, whose findings 369–432 are reconciled in §4 — all verified resolved/dismissed in the current tree)
**Tree:** Holosphere `e263b8d`
**Scope:** The Holosphere C++ engine and firmware — `core/` (rendering engine), `effects/` (28 effect headers incl. the shared `ReactionDiffusionBase.h`), `hardware/` (POV/DMA/sync drivers), `targets/` (WASM bridge + Phantasm entry), `tests/` (the native Clang suite), and the build/CI tooling (`CMakeLists.txt`, presets, toolchain, `.github/workflows`, `.githooks`, `scripts/`). ## 1. Executive Summary

Read cold against the current tree by twenty-two reviewers, the Holosphere engine holds the high bar the audit series has documented. **No critical defect, and no correctness bug that ships a wrong frame on the default single-instance render path, was found** — the eighth consecutive pass to reach that conclusion. The two High-severity items are both *latent*: a reachable NaN/inf when a Möbius-flow effect's live ring-count slider is driven to a degenerate value (433), and an unguarded canvas-width invariant on the Phantasm segmented ISR that would read out of bounds only if an effect ever used a non-`CANVAS_W` canvas (434). Neither fires on any shipped configuration today; both are one-line fail-fast guards away from closed.

The signature of this codebase — visible in nearly every reviewer's notes independently — is that its residual defects are overwhelmingly **internal inconsistencies with its own standards** rather than ordinary bugs. The engine's doctrine (fail-fast `HS_CHECK` on cold seams, stripped `assert` on hot paths backed by a cold trap, byte-documented arena budgets, wrapped trig phases, baked palettes, single-source-of-truth rosters) is applied so uniformly that the way you find a flaw is to look for the one place it *isn't*: the one free-function coordinate converter without the `H_VIRT>1` guard its templated sibling enforces (437), the one shape whose `distance()` writes per-pixel profiling counters while every sibling does not (438), the one Möbius animation that clamps one live divisor but not its twin (433), the one trail effect that uses `Orientation<4>` where its two documented siblings use `<16>` (455). When a codebase's defects are best described as exceptions to its own rules, the rules have largely won.

**The grade stays A− — the fifth consecutive audit at that mark — and this pass sharpens *where* the distance to a flat A lives.** It is almost entirely at the **verification frontier**, exactly as prior audits found, but this pass adds a new and uncomfortable item to it:

1. **A sim≠device determinism divergence the prior audits missed (447).** `hs::beat88` on the 64-bit host performs the FastLED phase arithmetic in 64-bit where the device wraps it modulo 2³², so `beat`-driven effects can render differently in the simulator than on hardware at realistic run times. The engine works hard everywhere else to make the WASM simulator bit-predict the device (`mt19937(1337)`, exact `sin8`/`scale8` mocks, fixed-point round-trips); this is the one host shim that quietly breaks that contract, and it sits beneath every `beatsin`-using effect.

2. **Test coverage is the single grade-limiting dimension (B+, the only sub-A average).** The suite is excellent where it reaches — independent oracles, a forked death harness that proves all 27 `HS_CHECK` traps fire with the exact illegal-instruction signal, a fault-injecting multi-board sync simulator — but the riskiest *new* math is unpinned: `WarpedVolume`/`Warp::Twist`'s analytic Lipschitz bound that keeps Raymarch's sphere-tracing safe (440), `DistortedRing`'s per-azimuth shift path (439), the reaction-diffusion physics (only smoke + determinism, no stability/positivity oracle — 449), the `ClipRegion` seam-wrap predicates the comments call "load-bearing" (436), and `DreamBalls`' 288-frame preset-cycle path which neither the 8-frame default nor the 120-frame CI smoke window reaches (450).

3. **The ninth audit's own resolutions introduced a small doc-hygiene regression (a Low cluster).** Several fix comments left bare tracker-id breadcrumbs (`finding 369`, `finding 408`, `finding 407`, `finding 323`) and contrastive "NOT-Y / deliberately not unified / Known divergences" phrasing in shipped source — both of which violate the project's own standing documentation conventions (describe current state; no rename/audit history). This is the audit series catching its own tail: the verification net is so thorough that the artifacts of verification have started leaking into the code.

**Verdict: A− overall.** The math/color/memory core, the hardware protocol layer, the WASM bridge, and the C++ test infrastructure each grade A or above on their own terms; the hardware sync subsystem and the build/CI engineering are A+. The distance to a flat A is the `beat88` determinism divergence (447), the two latent High guards (433, 434), the cluster of test-coverage gaps on the newest math, and the handful of capacity/invariant `static_assert`s and HS_CHECKs the engine elsewhere makes a habit of.

## 3. Component Assessments

**core: math** (`3dmath.h`, `util.h`, `constants.h`, `easing.h`, `waves.h`). High-quality, professionally-engineered numerical code. Primitives are edge-case-aware (slerp antipodal/near-identical branches, stereo/gnomonic pole sentinels with matched recognize thresholds, NaN-safe `fast_atan2` origin guard), hot-path conscious (squared-magnitude comparisons, branchless `wrap_t`, expanded 15-mul quaternion rotate), and the fast-vs-exact trig choice is made per call site with the drift consequence documented. `Vector` is deliberately kept trivially copyable for `memcpy` fast paths, with that dependency stated and enforced by `=default`. The grade is limited by `ClipRegion` in `constants.h`: a latent negative-modulo when `margin > x_start` (435), and the seam-wrap predicates the comments call load-bearing are untested (436).

**core: geometry / spatial / containers** (`geometry.h`, `spatial.h`, `static_circular_buffer.h`). Mathematically sound coord/LUT machinery (the split per-column-θ/per-row-φ `TrigLUT` achieves a documented ~145× memory reduction), careful arena/lifetime management, exemplary decision-site documentation (the `PhiLUT`/`TrigLUT` thread-safety contract, the `construct_in_place`/`std::launder` rationale), and disciplined hot/cold fail-fast separation. `StaticCircularBuffer` is genuinely STL-conformant and exception-safe. The one real inconsistency: the free-function `y_to_phi`/`phi_to_y(float,int)` divide by `(h_virt-1)` without the `H_VIRT>1` guard their templated siblings `static_assert` (437).

**core: sdf.** A clean shape concept, elegant generic CSG and `WarpedVolume<SDF,Warp>` composition (with an analytic operator-norm Lipschitz bound for safe sphere-tracing), and an unusually rigorous test suite that pins the accelerator paths (interval cull, anisotropic Face LUT) against exact oracles. The notable gaps are test coverage of the *newest* math — `DistortedRing`'s shift path (439), `WarpedVolume`/`Twist` (440), the `SmoothUnion` blend term (462) — and a per-pixel global-counter write in `Face::distance` that is both a hot-loop cost and an inconsistency with every other shape (438). A handful of degenerate-radius normalize sites (Star, SphericalPolygon) keep an unnormalized vector rather than a defined fallback (463, 464).

**core: scan.** A high-craft rasterizer: the scanline interval coalescer (seam-crossing spans wrapped, sorted, float-merged, and integer-clamped so each physical column is processed exactly once) and the `Volume` sphere-tracer are subtle and correct, with WHY-comments on nearly every tricky branch. Defects are minor and mostly latent: a NaN→int UB edge at the pole when `bounds_radius` is exactly 0 (466), a silently-evicting `norm` buffer whose safety rests on an un-asserted 32/64 capacity coupling (444), a wasted per-pixel rotation in `Volume` (467), and `static`-linkage function templates that work against the stated one-instantiation-per-`<W,H>` code-size goal (468). The main gap is coverage of `Volume`/`Mesh`/SSAA paths.

**core: plot.** A mature curve rasterizer reflecting deep domain understanding: adaptive `sinφ` stepping, a provably gap-free arc-aware clip cull (validated by a 6000-trial randomized bulge test with a non-vacuity counter), and clean separation of the two orthogonal interpolation axes (edge geodesic/planar vs `SplineMode` spline-eval) in code, comments, and README. The principal weakness is that the entire `rasterize()`/`draw()` control flow (adaptive stepping, cache backstop, `omitLast`, planar arc inversion, seam fallback) is explicitly untested (480). A null `FragmentShaderFn` is UB under `NDEBUG` at unguarded call sites (443).

**core: color / palettes.** The strongest-engineered component reviewed. A numerically careful, well-layered 16-bit linear pipeline whose subtle paths (saturating add, round-to-nearest `lerp16` proven bit-exact against a double-precision reference, OKLab hue rotation with `fast_cbrt` forward + exact inverse, clamp-before-cast UB discipline) are correct and exhaustively commented, with a clean two-world palette architecture (zero-overhead compile-time `StaticPalette` for the hot path, polymorphic `Palette`/`PaletteFacade` for cold bake/preset use). Residuals are all medium/low: the OKLab forward matrix duplicated between `linear_rgb_to_oklab` and `hue_rotate` (a hand-sync hazard, 441), a flat-vs-interpolated precision seam in `Gradient` (472), an unenforced extrapolation contract (473), and coverage gaps (ARM `uqadd16` 469, `Color4` alpha operators 470).

**core: animation / transformers.** An elegant type-erased timeline with CRTP animation types, compile-time-enforced borrow contracts (deleted rvalue overloads convert dangling-reference bugs into compile errors), a clean once-per-frame orientation-collapse pre-pass for multi-animation motion-blur composition, and outstanding rationale-driven documentation. The one substantive defect is `MobiusFlow` dividing by an unclamped live `num_rings` while clamping its sibling `num_lines` in the same function — a reachable NaN/inf (433, High). The rest are low-severity init/consistency nits (`Particle::life` uninitialized 474, `RandomTimer` no min≤max guard 478, the O(active²) collapse pre-pass 476).

**core: mesh / conway / hankin / solids.** High-craft embedded graphics code: the half-edge core and Conway operators are factored down to their genuine geometric differences through well-chosen shared scaffolds, every index-narrowing routes through `narrow_index`/`narrow_face_count` so a future budget bump traps rather than truncates, and the two load-bearing block comments (SCRATCH ARENA CONTRACT, COMPOSITION POLARITY) preserve the institutional knowledge that usually gets lost and then broken. Hankin pools are exact-sized to provable bounds with trapping guards. The main gaps are test-shaped: `classify_faces_by_topology` is exercised only on uniform meshes where its discriminating logic never runs (442), and `compile_hankin` only on the cube (482). `face_normal` dereferences before its `HE_NONE` guard, contradicting its own doc (though unreachable for the closed manifolds the operators produce — 475).

**core: reaction-graph / noise.** A small, well-factored spherical K-NN graph (analytic recompute-on-demand node generator + precomputed adjacency table + O(1) cubemap-seed LUT) paired with a cleanly-integrated vendored noise library. The depth of correctness reasoning in comments is the standout — the double-precision golden-angle accumulation that keeps the analytic generator bit-compatible with the offline table, and the `find_nearest_node` LOAD-BEARING note explaining why a "cleaner" refactor would silently return wrong nodes near the equator. Residuals: the `D_AVG` literal coupled to `RD_N` with no cross-check (471-adjacent), an off-lattice `lookup()` path the code's own comment flags as untested (483), and a vestigial `PROGMEM` marker no accessor honors (471).

**core: memory / platform.** Exemplary embedded engineering. The arena bounds arithmetic is a model of correct overflow-safe code; `random_to_unit`'s float-rounding-boundary handling is a genuinely subtle, unit-tested correctness fix; the `clamp` NaN→hi contract is defended end-to-end with a compile-time `#error` and two architecture-specific backends each documented with the reorder-sensitivity caveat. The one substantive defect is the `beat88` host/device width divergence that breaks the bit-exact determinism contract the rest of the file upholds (447). Plus a dead `owns_live()` predicate (485) and a theoretical `inplace_function` assignment exception-safety gap (486).

**core: filter / concepts / transformers.** A recursive variadic `Pipeline` with compile-time domain dispatch and type-system-encoded composition invariants (terminal-filter-last, world-before-screen ordering, flush-signature `requires` checks — all `static_assert`ed). The hot/cold discipline matches the doctrine precisely and the inline rationale is exceptional. The two findings that matter: a real out-of-range `trailFn` argument when `World::Trails::lifetime` is shrunk at runtime (the symmetric `age` path is clamped but `t` is not — 448), and the unenforced scratch-arena temporal-overlap contract between `Feedback::flush` and `Plot::rasterize`, a latent silent-corruption hazard the fail-fast model would otherwise want trapped (445).

**core: effect framework** (`canvas.h`, `effect_registry.h`, `effects.h`, `presets.h`, `styles.h`, `generators.h`, `led.h`). The double-buffer state machine, arena-lifecycle wrapper, self-registering factory, and feedback-style POD are all elegant and well-tested; the Canvas constructor's concurrency analysis (why the check-then-advance is not a TOCTOU race on a single-core Teensy, why relaxed atomics suffice) is exemplary, and the `HS_EFFECT_LIST` X-macro is a robust single source of truth cross-checked against the registry at startup. The two items worth attention: the shared static double-buffer's undocumented single-live-Effect precondition (446) and the untested `Style::sync_hue`/nonzero-`hue_fade` path (452).

**effects** (28 headers across four reviewer groups). Thin, well-documented orchestrators over a robust shared engine, faithful to the arena/fail-fast/declared-state philosophies, with consistently high comment quality (every magic constant and arena decision explained with *why*). No critical/high/medium defect in three of the four groups; the geometric and field groups carry medium test-coverage gaps. Cross-cutting themes: hand-duplicated trail scaffolding with documented live divergence (455, 456), a few cold-seam invariants guarded by stripped `assert` rather than `HS_CHECK` (453), and the tracker-id / `NOT-Y` comment-hygiene cluster the ninth audit's resolutions introduced (457, 458). `Dynamo`'s wipe lifecycle (address-stable boundary slots + FIFO reap, with a fully-documented memory-safe over-budget path) is a model of how to document a deliberate non-worst-case cap.

**hardware** (`dma_led.h`, `hd107s_frame.h`, `pov_single*.h`, `pov_segment_map.h`, `pov_segmented.h`, `pov_sync.h` + spec). The strongest area of the codebase. The single-board DMA stack carries a no-intermediate 16-bit-linear-to-wire path, branchless compile-time-folded index math, an eDMA 15-bit transfer-limit `static_assert`, and a wedged-DMA watchdog routed to the exact site a wedge manifests. The Phantasm sync subsystem — position-from-time flywheel with an integer rebase rule that makes the 32-bit cycle-counter wrap structurally unobservable, a single-writer mailbox/flywheel concurrency model, an odd-only distance-2 symbol alphabet with a plausibility gate, and a deadline-scheduled epoch commit — is professional-product quality, with a host test suite (event-driven multi-board simulator, ppm crystal skew, single-latch mask model, EMI/drop injection, one scenario per documented failure-budget row) to match. The one substantive defect is the missing effect-*width* invariant (434, High); the rest are documentation/containment nits (top-arm convention divergence 524-equivalent, telemetry torn read documented-benign).

**targets / wasm bridge** (`wasm.cpp`, `param_marshal.h`, `Phantasm.ino`, scripts). The cleanest seam in the project. The never-resize pixel-buffer contract (against `ALLOW_MEMORY_GROWTH` view detachment), the single-source param-marshal ordering, the X-macro resolution SSOT re-enumerated by the runtime smoke test, the generation-stamped tooling arenas, and the validate-before-teardown setters all verified correct, with compile-time `static_assert`s and startup `HS_CHECK`s enforcing every drift-prone coupling. Only low-severity items: copy-paste in the two spline bindings (568-equivalent) and the doubled `setEffect` X-macro dispatch, a missing OOM check on the Phantasm driver's `new` (599-equivalent), and a couple of tests/docs that slightly overstate their guarantees.

**tests** (C++ suite, two reviewer groups). An unusually disciplined regression suite: oracles are independent of the code under test (double-precision and brute-force references, round-trip invariants, Euler-characteristic checks, conservative-cull grids), the highest-risk paths (NaN/Inf domain safety, integer-overflow saturation, seam/pole edges, arena lifetime/compaction) are deliberately targeted, the release math-flag codegen is re-verified by a dedicated companion executable, and the death harness asserts all 27 traps fire with the exact signal. The `pov_sync` multi-board simulator is exceptional for a single-author project. Findings are test-rigor refinements: a capped distinct-buffer counter that can't detect a third-physical-buffer regression (459), a few oracles looser than the behavior they document (603-equivalent loosened tolerances), and the coverage gaps enumerated under §1.

**build / CI / toolchain.** Exceptionally well-engineered for a single-author project. Genuine single-sources-of-truth (the anchored CI shard matrix consumed by three jobs plus a shard-coverage gate that proves every test lands in exactly one shard), the float→int UB hazard defended in depth across three independent mechanisms (inline doc, compile-time `#error` keyed on `__FINITE_MATH_ONLY__`, and a runtime check compiled under the real release flags), a native-clang toolchain file that solves real Windows/emsdk papercuts cleanly, and provenance/supply-chain discipline (engine SHA + artifact sha256, dirty-tree guard, deploy gate consuming the trio). The only above-Low item is the Windows-only `cmd.exe` syntax in the `justfile` `docs` recipe (460), which contradicts the file's cross-platform framing.

### High

433. ✅ **MobiusFlow divides by a live, unclamped (num_rings + 1)** — `core/animation.h:1700`. step() computes logPeriod = 5.0f / (num_rings + 1) where num_rings is a std::reference_wrapper<const float> bound to a live (GUI-slider-backed) value, re-read every frame. If num_rings ever reaches -1.0f the result is inf, which feeds expf()->scale->s and produces inf/NaN in params a/d. The very same function deliberately clamps the sibling live value num_lines to >= 1 just below (lines 1707-1709) precisely to avoid a 2*PI/0 blow-up, with a comment citing 'the defensive floor in set_period/set_duration'. The num_rings divisor was left unguarded, an inconsistent omission of the identical defense. **Fix:** Clamp before dividing, mirroring the num_lines guard: float rings = num_rings; if (rings < 0.0f) rings = 0.0f; (or whatever floor keeps num_rings + 1 safely positive), then use rings in the logPeriod expression. At minimum guard against rings + 1 == 0. **Resolved:** step() now floors `rings = num_rings` at 0 before computing `logPeriod = 5.0f / (rings + 1)`, mirroring the sibling num_lines guard so the divisor stays >= 1.

434. ✅ **Effect canvas width is never validated against CANVAS_W; render_column can read out of bounds** — `hardware/pov_segmented.h:286-287, 580-608`. run_show() HS_CHECKs cur->height() == ROWS before taking an effect live, with an explicit comment that this guards the in-bounds indexing of buf[y * w + x_col]. But it never checks cur->width() == CANVAS_W. The sync engine derives the render column x from Config.W (= CANVAS_W passed at line 228), so x ranges over [0, CANVAS_W). render_column() then computes x_col = segment_x_col(arm_b_, x, w) with w = e->width() and indexes buf[y * w + x_col]. For an arm-A segment x_col == x; if an effect's width were less than CANVAS_W, x_col could equal or exceed w and the ISR would read past the row (and the row stride w would also mismatch the column count the flywheel sweeps), silently corrupting output on the hot path — the exact silent-corruption class the height check exists to prevent. In practice all current effects use CANVAS_W, so this is latent, but it is an unguarded invariant on the device hot path. **Fix:** Add HS_CHECK(cur->width() == CANVAS_W, "effect canvas width must equal CANVAS_W") right next to the existing height check at lines 286-287, before the flywheel ISR can take the instance live. **Resolved:** run_show() now HS_CHECKs `cur->width() == CANVAS_W` alongside the height check, trapping a width mismatch at the cold construction site before render_column's `buf[y * w + x_col]` can read past the row.

### Medium

435. ✅ **ClipRegion::render_x_start()/render_x_end() under-correct the cylindrical wrap when margin exceeds x_start (negative modulo result)** — `core/constants.h:46-51`. render_x_start() computes (x_start - margin + w) % w and render_x_end() computes (x_end + margin) % w. The single +w term only corrects one period of underflow. If margin > x_start + w (e.g. x_start=0, margin=10, w=8), x_start - margin + w = -2, and in C++ (-2) % 8 == -2, a negative column index that then propagates into contains_x()/x_clip()/XClip and ultimately into hot draw loops. The docstrings promise a result in [0, w). This is masked today only because all known callers keep margin < w (AntiAlias +/-1, MeshFeedback +/-8 vs hardware w=96), but there is no HS_CHECK or clamp enforcing margin < w, so a future high-margin filter would silently corrupt the clip band rather than trap. **Fix:** Either add a cold-path HS_CHECK(margin < w) where the region is configured, or make the wrap robust: use a modulo that always returns a non-negative result, e.g. ((x_start - margin) % w + w) % w (and likewise for the end), matching the documented [0, w) contract regardless of margin magnitude. **Resolved:** Took the cold-path guard to keep the per-fragment contains_x() path single-mod: Canvas::set_margin now HS_CHECKs `m < clip.w` at configuration time, and the render_x_start/end Doxygen documents the `margin < w` precondition the single-period wrap relies on.

436. ✅ **ClipRegion cylindrical x-clip logic (render_x_*, contains_x, x_clip/XClip) has no unit tests** — `core/constants.h:46-119`. Only could_intersect_y() is exercised (tests/test_plot_scan.h). The seam-crossing wrap interval in contains_x(), the rs==re full-width folding, and the XClip::clipped() wrap branch are explicitly documented as load-bearing ('hot loops can't accidentally blank a full segment') yet are untested. These are exactly the subtle cylindrical-wrap predicates where an off-by-one or a sign error (see the related negative-modulo finding) would silently blank or leak pixels at the seam without any test catching it. **Fix:** Add ClipRegion tests covering: a non-wrapping sub-band, a seam-crossing band (render_x_start > render_x_end), the wrap-to-full case (rs == re forcing active=false), full-width (x_end - x_start >= w), and parity between contains_x() and !XClip::clipped() over all columns for each case. **Resolved:** Added `test_clip_x_band_topologies` (tests/test_plot_scan.h), exercising all four band topologies at their edges plus a `contains_x()`/`!XClip::clipped()` parity sweep over every column (w = 96), so a sign error or off-by-one in either predicate now trips a test.

437. ✅ **Free-function y_to_phi/phi_to_y(float,int) divide by (h_virt-1) with no guard** — `core/geometry.h:174, 182-184`. y_to_phi(float y, int h_virt) returns (y*PI_F)/(h_virt-1) and phi_to_y(float phi, int h_virt) returns (phi*(h_virt-1))/PI_F. Neither guards h_virt. For h_virt==1 the former divides by zero (-> +/-inf), and for h_virt<=0 both produce garbage. The templated overloads (phi_to_y<H>, y_to_phi<H>) explicitly static_assert(H_VIRT>1) for exactly this degeneracy, so the free functions are inconsistent with the engine's own stated invariant and silently leak a non-finite phi into downstream trig/vector reconstruction instead of trapping. The test suite only exercises h_virt in {16,64,145}. **Fix:** Add an HS_CHECK(h_virt > 1) (cold path — these are setup/conversion helpers, not the per-pixel loop) at the top of both free overloads to match the templated guard, or document the precondition in the Doxygen and assert it. **Resolved:** Both free overloads now `HS_CHECK(h_virt > 1)` at entry (with a Doxygen note), matching the templated static_assert. Every current caller passes a compile-time `H_VIRT`, so the check constant-folds out under optimization — no hot-path cost on vector_to_pixel/PhiLUT::init.

438. ✅ **Face::distance writes global profiling counters on every pixel in the hot loop** — `core/sdf.h:1968-2013`. Face::distance() unconditionally increments hs::g_scan_metrics.pixels_tested and one of pixels_culled/lut_hits/exact_hits on every per-pixel evaluation. README 7.1 documents distance<>() as the per-pixel hot path, and the engineering-philosophy section is explicit that the per-pixel hot loop must stay lean. These are global-memory stores (a load-modify-store to a shared cache line per pixel) that no other shape's distance() performs, so Face is both slower than its siblings and inconsistent with them. The counters are plain non-atomic uint32_t globals; harmless today because rendering is main-loop only, but they would corrupt silently if any shading were ever moved off the main thread. **Fix:** Gate the metric increments behind a compile-time flag (e.g. HS_SCAN_METRICS) that is off in the device/release build, or hoist counting to the rasterizer (scan.h) so it is paid once per shape rather than per pixel. At minimum, make all shapes consistent (either all or none touch metrics). **Resolved:** Introduced `HS_SCAN_METRIC(stmt)` in core/platform.h, gated on a compile-time `HS_SCAN_METRICS` flag (undefined in the device/WASM release build → expands to nothing). All five Face::distance increments now go through it, so the per-pixel hot loop pays zero on device while the native test build (tests/CMakeLists.txt defines `HS_SCAN_METRICS`) keeps the lut_hits/exact_hits counters the Face LUT test reads. This also brings Face in line with the other shapes, which touch no metrics.

439. ✅ **DistortedRing::distance and the shift_fn path have no test coverage** — `core/sdf.h:658-684`. test_sdf.h covers Ring, PlanarPolygon, SphericalPolygon, Star, Line, Torus, all four CSG ops, AngularRepeat, and the Face LUT, but never constructs a DistortedRing or invokes its distance(). 440. ✅ **WarpedVolume and Warp::Twist (apply/lipschitz/correct_normal/bounding fast-path) are entirely untested** — `core/sdf.h:2805-2980`. The Twist warp carries the most intricate new math in the file (an analytic operator-norm Lipschitz bound 0.5*gamma + sqrt(1+0.25*gamma^2), and a chain-rule normal correction), and WarpedVolume::distance has a non-obvious bounding fast-path (return bd when bd > bounding_inflation, i.e. base distance > 2*inflation). None of it is covered by test_sdf.h. A wrong Lipschitz bound would let Raymarch's sphere tracing step through thin surfaces (the comment at 2851-2855 calls this out as the failure mode), which is exactly the kind of regression a test should pin. **Fix:** Add tests: (a) apply() displaces Y by amplitude*sin(twist*theta) at known points; (b) lipschitz returns 1 for twist==0 and matches the closed form for a sample gamma; (c) the WarpedVolume distance never exceeds raw_distance/lipschitz (sphere-trace safety); (d) correct_normal stays unit-length.

441. ✅ **norm-buffer overflow degrades silently instead of trapping (capacity coupling enforced only by comment)** — `core/scan.h:167-185`. scan_region builds a `StaticCircularBuffer<std::pair<float,float>, 64> norm` and may push two spans per input interval when a span crosses the x=0 seam. The code comment asserts safety because norm is '2x the 32-span input cap', but the two capacities (IntervalBuffer's 32 in sdf.h and the 64 literal here) are independent literals with no compile-time binding. Unlike push_interval(), which HS_CHECKs !is_full() and traps on overflow, norm.push_back silently evicts the OLDEST interval when full (StaticCircularBuffer's delete-oldest policy). If the interval cap is ever raised without raising 64, geometry is silently dropped on busy rows — exactly the failure mode the fail-fast philosophy exists to prevent, occurring in the one spot it is not applied. **Fix:** Bind the two capacities: derive the norm capacity as `2 * IntervalBuffer's N` (or a shared constexpr) and add `static_assert(norm_cap == 2 * interval_cap)`. Optionally HS_CHECK(!norm.is_full()) before each push so an overflow traps rather than evicts, matching push_interval.

442. ✅ **Point::draw calls fragment_shader unconditionally; null shader is only a debug assert, so a null FragmentShaderFn corrupts/UB on device** — `core/plot.h:86`. Point::draw invokes fragment_shader(f_copy.pos, f_copy) with no bool check. FragmentShaderFn is a FunctionRef whose operator() only asserts non-null in debug (concepts.h:148-150); under NDEBUG a default-constructed/empty shader calls thunk_(nullptr,...) through a null function pointer — undefined behavior on the device. The same unguarded pattern repeats in rasterize()'s process_segment (lines 398, 410, 416, 472, 497) and Vertices::draw (line 704). Whether this is reachable depends on callers always passing a real shader; that contract is not enforced or documented at these call sites. **Fix:** Document the non-null FragmentShaderFn precondition on the rasterize/draw entry points, or add an always-on HS_CHECK(fragment_shader) at the rasterize() entry (cold, once per call, not per-pixel) so a null shader traps deterministically instead of invoking a null thunk under NDEBUG.

443. ✅ **Mesh face-index reads (u/v) are not bounds-checked before mesh.vertices[u] when u is the smaller index** — `core/plot.h:1716`. process_edge guards only `large < mesh.vertices.size()` (HS_CHECK at 1716) before reading mesh.vertices[u] (1719) and mesh.vertices[v] (1721). Since large = max(u,v), large in-bounds implies both u and v are in-bounds, so this is actually safe — BUT the dedup-capacity guard at 1707 (`large < kDedupCapacity`) is what bounds large, and the comment claims vertices[] 'only asserts in bounds (stripped under NDEBUG)'. If a face index is negative (fi is uint16_t so it cannot be, but u/v are widened to int and a malformed uint16 of e.g. 65535 passes only if 65535 < vertices.size()), the small index is never independently validated. With uint16_t face data and vertices.size() typically < 65535 the large-index check covers it, so this is defensive only. **Fix:** Either note in the comment that 'large in-bounds implies small in-bounds because indices are non-negative', or assert both endpoints explicitly (as the precomputed-edge overload at line 1845 already does for u AND v) for symmetry and clarity.

444. ✅ **OKLab forward matrix coefficients duplicated between linear_rgb_to_oklab and hue_rotate** — `core/color.h:554-562 and 611-618`. The linear-RGB -> LMS matrix (0.4122214708 ... 0.6299787005) and the LMS' -> OKLab matrix (0.2104542553 ... -0.8086757660) are written out verbatim twice: once in linear_rgb_to_oklab() and again inline in hue_rotate(). hue_rotate inlines them only so it can substitute fast_cbrt for cbrtf, but the linear coefficients are identical. A future correction or precision tweak to one copy will silently diverge from the other, producing a hue_rotate that no longer matches the canonical OKLab conversion — a class of bug that is invisible until someone notices colors drift only under the feedback/Flyby path. **Fix:** Factor the two matrix multiplies into small constexpr helpers (e.g. linear_rgb_to_lms() returning the (l,m,s) triple, and lms_to_oklab() taking the cube-rooted triple) and have both linear_rgb_to_oklab and hue_rotate call them, passing cbrtf vs fast_cbrt as the nonlinearity. This removes the hand-sync hazard with zero runtime cost (all inline).

445. ✅ **classify_faces_by_topology lacks a test with genuinely distinct face classes** — `tests/test_mesh.h:348-380`. Both classifier tests use a cube and a tetrahedron — meshes where every face is topologically identical, so the expected output is trivially all-zeros. A pass would still occur even if the neighbor-hash folding, the heap-sort of HashNode, and the dense current_id++ assignment (mesh.h:676-685) were completely broken (e.g. if every face hashed identically by accident). The discriminating logic — the part most likely to harbor a bug — is never exercised. A mixed-face solid (e.g. a truncated tetrahedron: triangles + hexagons, or any Archimedean solid) would assert that the classifier produces >1 distinct class and groups like faces together. **Fix:** Add a test on a multi-face-type solid (e.g. truncate(tetrahedron) or a cuboctahedron) asserting the expected number of distinct topology classes and that faces of the same shape share an id.

446. ✅ **Host beat88 uses 64-bit arithmetic where the device uses 32-bit, breaking the bit-exact determinism contract** — `core/platform.h:714-716`. beat88 computes ((hs::millis() - timebase) * bpm88 * 280) >> 16. hs::millis() returns `unsigned long`, which is 64-bit on the LP64/host build, so the multiply (and the value being shifted) is performed in 64 bits. FastLED's device beat88 performs this in 32-bit (GET_MILLIS() is uint32_t, accum88 is 16-bit) and intentionally wraps modulo 2^32 before the >>16. Because hs::millis() is steady_clock-since-epoch (a large absolute value, not a small per-effect delta), the host product exceeds 2^32 almost immediately, so the host and device produce different phase values whenever (millis-timebase)*bpm88*280 wraps a uint32 on the device but not on the 64-bit host. This silently violates the determinism contract that the rest of platform.h works hard to uphold (the contract is even called out for sin8/scale8). beat16/beat8/beatsin8/beatsin16 all route through this, so any beat-driven effect can render differently in the simulator vs the device at realistic run times. **Fix:** Force 32-bit modular arithmetic to match the device: compute with explicit uint32_t operands, e.g. `return (uint32_t)((uint32_t)(hs::millis() - timebase) * (uint32_t)bpm88 * 280u) >> 16;` so the host wraps identically to FastLED's 32-bit path. Add a beat88 test pinning a large mock millis() value that wraps the 32-bit product. **Resolution (false positive — no behavioral divergence):** validated and found incorrect. beat88 returns `(P >> 16)` narrowed to `uint16_t`, i.e. bits [16,31] of the product P. The device computes `P mod 2^32`, which only discards bits >= 32 — bits [16,31] are untouched — so the device's 16-bit output equals the host's 64-bit output. Moreover `P mod 2^32 = (low32(millis)·K) mod 2^32`, so the host's larger 64-bit `millis` influences the result only through its low 32 bits, exactly what the device sees. Empirically confirmed across many millis values (e.g. `millis=1.7e12 → 37376` both ways; `123456789012 → 40641` both ways; full sweeps all EQUAL). The proposed regression test cannot be written: there is no millis value where the 32-bit and 64-bit paths differ after the `>>16` + uint16 narrowing (a non-vacuity guard on the attempted test fired, proving this). The arithmetic width is also already 32-bit on the two builds that matter — wasm32 and the device are both ILP32 (`unsigned long` = 32-bit); only the LP64 native test build does the multiply in 64 bits, and even there the output is identical. The explicit-uint32 cast is harmless but a no-op, so it was not applied. *Note:* this contradicts the executive summary's headline (#1) and parts of the A− grade rationale, which still cite 446 as the lone determinism breach; those narrative sections are left as-is pending the author's review of this resolution.

447. ✅ **World::Trails::flush passes a possibly out-of-range t into trailFn after set_lifetime() shrink** — `core/filter.h:768-779`. When set_lifetime() lowers `lifetime` below the encoded ttl of an already-buffered item, t = 1.0f - (item.ttl / lifetime) becomes negative (ttl > lifetime). The code explicitly handles the symmetric problem for the re-emitted age (clamps `age = lifetime - ttl` to >= 0 at lines 776-778, with a comment naming exactly this set_lifetime() race) but feeds the un-clamped negative t straight into trailFn(v, t) at line 770. WorldTrailFn implementations are documented to receive t in [0,1] (fade progress); a negative t can index a palette/gradient out of range or produce a nonsensical color. The age path proves the author is aware of the hazard but the t path was missed. **Fix:** Clamp t to [0,1] before the trailFn call (e.g. `float t = hs::clamp(1.0f - ttl/lifetime, 0.0f, 1.0f);`), mirroring the existing age clamp a few lines below, and add a test that shrinks lifetime via set_lifetime() while older items are buffered.

448. ✅ **Scratch-arena sharing between Feedback::flush and Plot::rasterize is a temporal-non-overlap contract with no compile/runtime guard** — `core/filter.h:1234-1251`. Feedback::flush opens a ScratchScope on scratch_arena_a and keeps dx/dy live for the whole function; the comment notes Plot::rasterize also checkpoints scratch_arena_a and that safety depends ENTIRELY on flush() and any plot()/rasterize using the same arena never overlapping in time (enforced today only by the convention that every effect runs flush() and draw as separate phases). This is a real aliasing hazard: a future filter that allocates from scratch_arena_a inside its own plot() path and interleaves with a feedback flush would silently corrupt the warp field (or vice-versa) with no trap — exactly the silent-corruption failure mode the project's fail-fast philosophy targets. The contract is documented but unenforced. **Fix:** Add a cheap runtime sentinel that fail-fast-traps on re-entrant/overlapping checkpoints of scratch_arena_a (e.g. an `in_use` flag set by ScratchScope on this arena, HS_CHECK'd on a second concurrent scope), so a future interleaving faults loudly instead of corrupting pixels. At minimum, assert the invariant rather than relying solely on the prose contract. **Resolved:** The proposed `in_use` sentinel is unsound — scratch_arena_a is a LIFO bump allocator and legitimately holds multiple stack-nested scopes at once (Plot::rasterize alone opens `_sc`/`_frag`/`_edge`/`_trail` on it), so trapping the second concurrent scope would fire on normal rendering. Nested same-arena scopes are in fact safe BY CONSTRUCTION: each inner scope saves a mark above the live allocations and rewinds to exactly where they end. The only way to corrupt the warp field is non-LIFO teardown — a scope rewinding while an enclosing one is still live — which leaves the arena offset below the inner scope's saved mark. `~ScratchScope` now HS_CHECKs `arena.get_offset() >= saved_offset`, trapping that non-LIFO case directly (the structural enforcement of the contract); a `scratch_scope_non_lifo` death case pins it. The load-bearing comments at filter.h flush and plot.h rasterize were rewritten to state the real invariant (LIFO discipline + no raw arena pointer outliving its scope) instead of the overstated "never overlap in time" prose.

449. ✅ **Shared static buffers couple all Effect instances with no enforced single-effect invariant** — `core/canvas.h:445-449`. buffer_a and buffer_b are `static DMAMEM Pixel[]` members, so every Effect instance points bufs_[0]/bufs_[1] at the same two physical arrays (confirmed: core/memory.cpp:129/131 defines them once). The whole double-buffer correctness argument assumes exactly one live Effect. Today that holds (WASM does currentEffect.reset() before create_effect, targets run one effect), but nothing in Effect enforces it: two simultaneously-constructed Effects would silently scribble over each other's pixels and each other's prev_/cur_/next_ would index a shared array, producing torn frames with no diagnostic. The doc comment ('Static storage for buffer A') does not warn about this single-instance precondition. **Fix:** Document the one-live-Effect precondition prominently at the buffer_a/buffer_b declarations, and consider a fail-fast guard (e.g. a static `bool s_alive` set in the ctor / cleared in the dtor with HS_CHECK on double-construction) so an accidental overlapping lifetime traps instead of corrupting silently.

450. ✅ **Style::sync_hue() and the hue_fade cos/sin cache path are untested** — `tests/test_styles.h:169-179`. hue_fade reads the frame-constant hue_ca/hue_sa cache populated by sync_hue(). The styles suite only tests hue_fade at hue_shift=0 (test_hue_fade_zero_shift_preserves_gray), which exercises the identity rotation (hue_ca=1, hue_sa=0, the struct default) and never calls sync_hue() at all. 451. ✅ **RD physics dynamics have no unit-test coverage; only smoke + cross-run determinism integration tests exist** — `effects/GSReactionDiffusion.h:60-66, 160-180`. 589. ✅ **update_hankin's zero-length-edge fallback branch is not covered** — `tests/test_hankin.h:123-144`. test_update_hankin_flat_collapses_to_corners covers the angle==0 branch (dynamic vertex -> normalized corner), and the populate/clone/clear tests cover the normal twisted path. But the second collapse-to-corner path in update_hankin (core/hankin.h:313-317), taken when an input edge is degenerate (dot(cross1,cross1) < EPS_CROSS_SQ), is never exercised: every test feeds a clean cube whose edges are all non-degenerate. A regression that mis-handled a degenerate-edge corner (e.g. emitting NaN instead of the normalized corner) on a real malformed mesh would not be caught. *Fix:* Add a small fixture with two coincident/near-coincident adjacent corner vertices (a degenerate edge) at a non-zero angle and assert the corresponding dynamic vertex collapses to the normalized corner and stays finite/unit-length. **Resolved:** added test_update_hankin_degenerate_edge_collapses_to_corner — it compiles a clean cube, forces one instruction's previous corner to coincide with its corner (so cross(p_prev,p_corner)==0), runs update_hankin at a non-zero angle (0.5), and asserts the dynamic vertex equals the normalised corner and is finite + unit-length. Without the guard this path normalises a zero vector and emits NaN, so the test genuinely exercises the branch.

590. **Complex-Islamic determinism only checks the first registry entry, not the family** — `tests/test_solids.h:301-305`. test_determinism_complex_islamic calls check_determinism_for_index(base) — the FIRST islamic entry only. The islamic generators are the deepest Conway-op chains (the most likely to introduce order/RNG-dependent nondeterminism), and the comment ('the longest path') implies the worst case is covered, but only one of N islamic entries is actually re-built and diffed. A nondeterministic op reached only by a later islamic entry would slip through. *Fix:* Loop check_determinism_for_index over all islamic indices (or at least a representative spread including the longest chain), mirroring the registry-integrity loops in the same file.

591. **Comment/code mismatch: 'inverted range -> min' for random(9,4) describes mock-only behavior as Arduino-faithful** — `tests/test_platform.h:157-161`. test_random_degenerate_range asserts random(9,4)==9 with the doc claiming the device matches. Arduino's random(min,max) with min>max does not have a documented 'return min' contract (its long-range behavior with an inverted span is effectively unspecified / can produce a value via the negative span). The test correctly pins the HOST MOCK's chosen behavior, which is the right thing to do, but the surrounding rationale presents it as 'the device returns the floor', overstating cross-device guarantees for the inverted case (the degenerate empty-range random(5,5)==5 case is the genuinely well-defined one). *Fix:* Reword the inverted-range comment to state this is the mock's defined contract (chosen to avoid SIGFPE/UB), not a behavior the real Arduino runtime guarantees; keep the empty-range (min==max) case framed as the device-faithful one.

592. **test_node_deterministic_and_distinct relies on a hard-coded loop bound staying below RD_N-1** — `tests/test_reaction_graph.h:68-74`. The loop runs i in [0,500) and reads node(i+1), i.e. up to node(500). This is safe only while RD_N (7680) stays well above 501. node() is documented as analytic over [0,RD_N), so node(500) is fine today, but the bound is a magic constant with no relation to RD_N; if RD_N were ever reduced below 501 (or the lattice contract changed) the i+1 access becomes the silent edge case the rest of the file is careful to guard. *Fix:* Clamp the loop to `i < std::min(500, RD_N - 1)` (or iterate to RD_N-1) so the adjacency read can never run past the lattice regardless of RD_N.

593. **Duplicated MockMesh definition across two death cases** — `tests/test_death.h:451-491, 511-549`. case_plot_mesh_vertex_over_capacity and case_plot_extract_edges_vertex_over_capacity each define a near-identical local MockMesh (same Verts stub, same fc{2}/fi{0,130}, same accessors). The two copies must be kept in sync by hand; a change to the duck-typed mesh interface (e.g. a new required accessor) would need editing in both places, and a divergence would silently weaken one case. *Fix:* Hoist a single shared over-capacity MockMesh into a detail namespace in test_death.h (or mesh_test_util.h) and reuse it in both death cases.

594. **ccache CMAKE_CXX_COMPILER_LAUNCHER is set for the Emscripten build but the wasm CI job never installs ccache into the link path for em++, and a launcher-vs-emcc mismatch can silently disable caching** — `CMakeLists.txt:15-19`. find_program(ccache) sets CMAKE_CXX_COMPILER_LAUNCHER unconditionally for both modes. For the WASM build the actual compiler is em++ (a Python wrapper); ccache fronting em++ works but is fragile across emsdk versions, and the project itself acknowledges the uncertainty by adding 'ccache stats' steps that say 'zero hits means the integration regressed'. This is defensive but the wasm job does not FAIL on a zero-hit run, so a silent regression to no-caching only degrades perf, never breaks the build. Acceptable, but the self-described verification is advisory only. *Fix:* Optionally assert a nonzero hit rate (or at least a nonzero ccache invocation count) in the 'ccache stats' steps on cache-warm runs so the documented 'dead weight' condition actually fails rather than being eyeballed.

595. **Pre-commit hook trigger grep omits justfile, ci.yml, and ubsan-ignorelist.txt, so build-infra-only commits skip the suite** — `.githooks/pre-commit:37`. The change-detection grep matches C++ sources, CMakeLists.txt, CMakePresets.json, and anything under cmake/. It does NOT match justfile, .github/workflows/ci.yml, .githooks/*, or tests/ubsan-ignorelist.txt. A commit that, say, breaks the shard regex in ci.yml or edits the ignorelist will skip the local suite. This is largely fine (CI re-runs from the committed tree and is authoritative, as the hook header notes), but a change to tests/ubsan-ignorelist.txt directly affects what the sanitizer build suppresses and arguably should run the suite locally. *Fix:* If desired, extend the grep to include (^|/)tests/ubsan-ignorelist\.txt$; the workflow/justfile cases are reasonably left to CI. Not a correctness defect given CI is the authoritative gate.

596. **_hs_test_modules (CMake) and kModules (run_tests.cpp) are two hand-maintained lists with no automated equality assertion** — `tests/CMakeLists.txt:90-97`. The 32-entry module list in tests/CMakeLists.txt must stay in lockstep with the kModules roster in tests/run_tests.cpp. They are currently identical (verified). The shard-coverage CI job asserts every CMake-registered test maps to exactly one shard, and an unknown-module argv fails run_tests with exit 2, so a CMake entry with no roster counterpart WOULD fail CI. But a roster module that is never registered in CMake (the reverse direction) runs in no CTest test and is silently uncovered — nothing asserts the run_tests roster is a subset of the registered modules. *Fix:* Consider a tiny CI check that runs 'run_tests --list' and diffs against the ctest -N module set, closing the reverse-direction gap. Low priority since additions normally touch both files together.

597. **Debug WASM preset is compile-checked but never run, so -sASSERTIONS / -sSTACK_SIZE=65536 runtime behavior is unverified** — `.github/workflows/ci.yml:233-240`. The wasm-debug job is explicitly compile-only (the step comment says so) to prevent bit-rot. That is a reasonable cost tradeoff, but it means the debug-only 64 KB stack sizing and -sASSERTIONS path are never actually instantiated/run; a debug-only assertion that aborts at instantiation or a stack budget that is still too small under -O0 would not be caught. CMakeLists.txt line 56 also states 'The debug preset is never exercised by CI or the smoke script', which is consistent with this being a deliberate accepted gap. *Fix:* No action required given the documented tradeoff; if cheap, the existing wasm_smoke.mjs could be pointed at the debug .js in a follow-on step to also exercise the assertions path.

598. **install(CODE) git rev-parse failure path writes no .sha but still proceeds to write the .wasm.sha256, producing a partial provenance set** — `CMakeLists.txt:125-136`. If 'git rev-parse HEAD' fails (HS_GIT_RC != 0), the code emits a WARNING and skips writing the .sha, but unconditionally proceeds to SHA256 the .wasm and write .wasm.sha256. The result is a provenance pair (.wasm + .wasm.sha256) without the .sha the deploy gate needs. The deploy gate will then fail on the missing .sha (as the warning predicts), so this is fail-loud at the consumer, not silent corruption — but the install step itself returns success while having produced an incomplete trio. The CI 'Exercise install + provenance path' step checks all three exist, so this is caught in CI; it is purely a local-build sharp edge. *Fix:* Optionally make the rev-parse failure a FATAL_ERROR in the install CODE (or skip writing the .wasm.sha256 too) so a local install never silently yields a half-written provenance set. Minor.

599. **Stray untracked 'grep' file and Doxyfile.local are not gitignored** — `.gitignore:n/a`. git status shows two untracked files: an empty 'grep' (almost certainly an accidental shell redirection artifact) and 'Doxyfile.local' (the local Doxygen config the justfile 'docs' recipe feeds to doxygen). Neither is ignored. Doxyfile.local in particular is a machine-local artifact analogous to CMakeUserPresets.json (already ignored) and the .doxygen-awesome/ clone (already ignored), so it is inconsistent that it is not. Risk is only of accidentally committing local cruft. *Fix:* Add /Doxyfile.local to .gitignore alongside the other machine-local/tooling entries, and delete the stray empty 'grep' file. Not a build-infra source defect, but the gitignore is in scope.

600. **find_program(CCACHE_FOUND ccache) variable name reads as a boolean but holds the path** — `CMakeLists.txt:15-18`. CCACHE_FOUND is set by find_program to the ccache executable PATH (or CCACHE_FOUND-NOTFOUND), then used both as the if() truthiness test and as the launcher value. This works correctly, but the _FOUND suffix conventionally denotes a boolean, while this variable is the path. A reader could mistake it for a flag. Purely a readability nit in otherwise exemplary CMake. *Fix:* Rename to CCACHE_PROGRAM (CMake's own convention for find_program results) for clarity; behavior is unchanged.

601. ✅ **HD107SFrame flushes a TX-only DMA buffer with clean+invalidate when a clean alone suffices** — `hardware/hd107s_frame.h:169,207`. load() and flush() call arm_dcache_flush_delete() (clean + invalidate) on buffer_, but the buffer is TX-only: the CPU writes it (constructor/load/packPixel) and the eDMA channel only reads it (wired to LPSPI4 TX; dma_led.h:204). For a CPU→DMA buffer only the clean (write-back) is required; the invalidate needlessly evicts cache lines the CPU re-reads and re-writes the very next frame, costing an extra read-allocate miss per line. Correctness is fine either way given the 32-byte alignment — purely a small efficiency note. *Fix:* Use arm_dcache_flush() (clean, no invalidate) at both call sites and update the host no-op stub accordingly; the lines stay resident for the next frame's packPixel/load. *(Resolved: renamed the intrinsic and host stub to arm_dcache_flush and switched both flush points to clean-only; docs in hd107s_frame.h, dma_led.h, and README updated to explain the TX-only rationale.)*

### Cross-cutting tracked risk

602. ✅ **"Device-only divergence invisible to the host suite" is a comment motif, not a tracked risk** — `core/filter.h:937`, `tests/test_solids.h:321`, `tests/test_filter.h:262`, `tests/test_h_offset_renorm.h`. The phrase (and close variants) recurs verbatim across the source. The test strategy is excellent for platform-agnostic logic but has a systematic blind spot wherever behavior forks on a `CORE_TEENSY`/`ARDUINO`-only constant: a normal host build compiles the host value, so the device path can ship with zero automated coverage. The honest comments named the hazard one site at a time but never aggregated it into a coverage view, so it was impossible to answer "which device-only forks are reached by a device-value test, and which are not?" *Severity: Medium (systemic test-strategy visibility gap).* **Resolved:** Inventoried every such fork into a single coverage ledger — [docs/device_host_divergence_ledger.md](device_host_divergence_ledger.md) — each row tagged with whether a device-value test build reaches it, plus a maintenance rule that a new device-only fork must add a row. The ledger surfaces exactly one red row: `beat88` (finding 446), the lone behavioral fork with no device-value test, which the ledger now tracks as the standing risk to close. The `H_OFFSET` renorm fold (now covered by the `h_offset_renorm` device-value build) and the Conway 120 KB scratch budget (host high-water asserted against the device value) are recorded green.

603. ✅ **Blur drops the off-pole kernel row without renormalizing, darkening the pole rows; AntiAlias renormalizes the same clip** — `core/filter.h:1124-1145`. Screen::Blur::plot skips any 3x3 row whose `ny` falls off the pole (poles don't wrap, so the sink would clip it anyway) via `k += 3`, but the dropped row's kernel weight is simply lost — the surviving taps then sum to < 1 and the edge rows deposit < alpha, dimming the pole rows by a few percent at full blur. AntiAlias faces the identical pole clip and renormalizes its Y split (lines 946-962) by folding the clipped row's weight into the survivors. A quality nit rather than a bug, but an inconsistency between two filters in the same situation. *Fixed:* Blur now scales surviving taps by 1/(sum of surviving-row weights). Interior rows clip nothing, so the divisor is the full kernel (1) and the scale is exactly 1.0f — the per-tap multiply is a no-op there, keeping the common path bit-identical; the renorm branch only runs on the two pole rows. Added `test_blur_pole_row_renormalizes` asserting a pole-row tap deposits the full input alpha across the 6 surviving taps with none out of bounds.

604. ✅ **animation test harness leaks a process-lifetime Effect, tripping the single-live-Effect guard in any later module of an all-modules run** — `tests/test_animation.h:91-95`. `fake_canvas()` backed its shared stand-in Canvas with a function-local `static FakeEffect`. A `static` Effect's destructor only runs at process exit, so once the animation module touched it the `canvas.h` `s_alive` guard stayed set for the rest of the process; the next module's first `Effect` construction then aborted with `HS_CHECK(!s_alive)` ("a second Effect was constructed while one is still alive"). The per-module ctest gate runs one module per process (`add_test … COMMAND run_tests <module>`), so it never observed this — but the bare `run_tests` binary with no args (all modules in one process) died at the start of the `effects` module. A latent test-harness hygiene defect, not a shipping-frame bug, but it made the all-in-one-process run unusable and masked any real Effect-lifetime regression a single combined run would catch. **Resolved:** Moved the fixture from a process-lifetime static to a module-scoped `FakeEffect`/`Canvas` owned by `run_animation_tests()` and published via `fake_canvas_ptr()`; destroying it on return releases the guard so the following module starts clean. The single-Canvas (no second `queue_frame`) coupling is preserved, the production guard is untouched (still fully active in every build), and the bare all-modules run now completes 0-failure across all 32 modules.

## 6. Overall Impression & Appraisal

*This section is an independent assessment of the project's merit and standing. It is deliberately **not** based on the prior review.*

### The magnitude of the achievement

Holosphere is a single author's rendering engine that runs, from one body of C++, on two radically different machines: a 600 MHz Cortex-M7 with 512 KB of RAM spinning a 40-LED strip at 480 RPM, and a WebAssembly module driving a 288×144 instanced Three.js sphere in a browser. It is not a toy that happens to compile twice. It is a genuine graphics engine — SDF rasterization with analytic anti-aliasing, a geodesic/planar curve rasterizer with latitude-adaptive sampling, a three-domain (world/screen/pixel) compile-time filter pipeline, a half-edge mesh kernel with the full Conway operator algebra, Hankin Islamic-pattern synthesis, a spherical reaction-diffusion solver on a Fibonacci K-NN graph, OKLCH perceptual color throughout, and a partitioned arena allocator with zero per-frame heap — and then, beneath all of that, a four-board persistence-of-vision firmware coordinated over a *single wire* by a count-coded symbol protocol with a flywheel timebase, an acceptance gate, and a fail-dark epoch-commit scheme. Any one of those subsystems would be a respectable standalone project. The achievement is that they coexist in one coherent, mutually reinforcing design, held to a uniform engineering standard, and — this is the rare part — *verified*: a native test suite with independent oracles, a death harness that proves the fail-fast traps actually fire, a fault-injecting multi-board sync simulator, a runtime WASM smoke test driving every effect at both resolutions, and a SHA-verified deploy gate.

The scale is real: ~50,000 lines of first-party C++ (excluding vendored/generated data), 28 effects, 52 registered polyhedra, ~15k lines of tests. But scale is the least impressive thing about it. The impressive thing is the *consistency of judgment* across that scale — that the same person made the same quality of decision in `core/3dmath.h`'s quaternion rotate, in `hardware/pov_sync.h`'s epoch scheduler, and in `CMakeLists.txt`'s provenance trio.

### Technical merit

On pure engineering craft this sits in the top percentile of code I would expect to review in any context. The hallmarks:

- **Documentation of *why*, at the line that depends on it.** Measured error bounds on every fast approximation; a written non-race proof on the double buffer; the `lerp16` shift-reconstruction derivation; the COMPOSITION-POLARITY and SCRATCH-ARENA contracts that explicitly warn future maintainers against "standardizing" a deliberate asymmetry. This is the documentation style of someone who has been burned by losing institutional knowledge and decided not to let it happen again.
- **A coherent error philosophy, consistently applied.** Fail-fast `HS_CHECK` on cold seams (stdio-free, survives `NDEBUG`, death-harness-verified); stripped `assert` on hot paths backed by a cold trap at the setup site; bounded soft-handling for genuinely transient conditions; log-and-reject at the untrusted WASM boundary. Most codebases pick one and misapply it everywhere; this one picks the right tool per seam and documents the choice.
- **Sim-predicts-device determinism as a design goal.** Identical RNG seed, bit-exact integer mocks, fixed-point round-trips — the simulator is built to *predict* the hardware, not approximate it. (Finding 447 is notable precisely because it is the lone breach of a contract pursued this seriously.)
- **Verification that reaches the hard parts.** The conservative-cull oracle, the Euler-characteristic mesh checks, the 6000-trial bulge test with a non-vacuity counter, the release-flag codegen re-verified under the real `-ffast-math` line, and a sync protocol tested with crystal skew and EMI injection against a per-row failure-mode budget. This is testing as engineering, not as ritual.

Where it is weakest is the obverse of its strength: the newest, most numerically delicate code (the `Twist` Lipschitz bound, the reaction-diffusion stability margin, the planar-arc registers) tends to ship documented-but-unpinned, so the test net trails the frontier of the code by one release. That is the entire substance of the A− vs A gap, and it is a frontier problem, not a quality problem.

### Artistic merit

The effects are not tech demos with a palette bolted on. They draw from a deliberately deep well — Islamic geometric pattern synthesis via Hankin's actual method on Archimedean solids, the Hopf fibration with a 4D tumble, spherical harmonics with perceptual lobe-polarity shading, Belousov-Zhabotinsky and Gray-Scott reaction-diffusion on a sphere, loxodromic Möbius flows, spherical Voronoi. The OKLCH-everywhere color discipline and 16-bit linear compositing mean the gradients and multi-layer alpha actually look right rather than muddy — a choice most LED-art codebases never make. And the whole thing is authored for a *physical* medium: a glowing sphere of light in a room, where motion blur from a 480 RPM sweep is a feature the engine models rather than an artifact it fights. The geometry tools (Lissajous/Möbius/palette/solids/splines designers, the last two backed by the same WASM engine code so the tool can't lie about what the firmware will do) show an artist building their own instruments. This is craft in the old sense — the maker made the tools that made the work.

### Standing relative to the field

- **Versus hobbyist / open-source LED art** (FastLED demos, WLED, the Adafruit ecosystem, Pixelblaze patterns): not a comparison. Those are overwhelmingly 8-bit-sRGB, single-target, single-file pattern collections with little or no testing. Holosphere is a different category of artifact — an engine with an architecture, a test suite, and CI — and would be the most sophisticated project by a wide margin in any of those communities.
- **Versus professional embedded/firmware practice:** the fail-fast doctrine, the host-testable pure-core/device-shell split, the provenance-verified deploy gate, and the determinism contract are at or above what I would expect from a competent professional firmware team, and the *documentation* is well above typical industry practice. A pro team would likely have broader test coverage on the newest features (the gap this audit keeps flagging) and more process around it — but would not obviously write better code.
- **Versus professional graphics engines:** scope is incomparable (no asset pipeline, no general material system, no GPU shader graph — it doesn't need them). But on the axes it does occupy — zero-overhead compile-time specialization, a clean rasterizer/shader interface, perceptual color done correctly — the individual subsystems are professional-grade. The filter pipeline and the SDF/scan split would not look out of place in a shipping renderer.
- **Versus academic research:** the engine *applies* known results (Hankin's construction, OKLab/Ottosson, Gray-Scott, the Hopf map, Conway operators, Fibonacci-lattice K-NN) rather than advancing them — this is not a research contribution and does not claim to be. But the *synthesis* — running reaction-diffusion and Islamic-pattern synthesis and a Möbius-warped Hopf fibration through one arena-allocated, compile-time-resolution engine onto a spinning POV sphere — is something I have not seen assembled anywhere, in research or industry. The Phantasm one-wire multi-board sync protocol, in particular, is a genuinely clever piece of systems design (count-coded, distance-2, fail-to-missed-never-wrong, with a flywheel that turns interrupt-mask windows into free coasts) that is documented to a standard a conference paper would accept.
- **Versus the state of the art in generative/creative coding** (Shadertoy, three.js examples, TouchDesigner/VJ tooling): those reach further visually because they ride the GPU and a mature host. Holosphere's distinction is the opposite constraint — producing comparable visual sophistication under a hard real-time, no-heap, microsecond-budget embedded ceiling, *and* mirroring it faithfully in the browser. As an instance of "serious graphics engineering aimed at a bespoke physical display," it is at the high end of what individuals produce.

### Bottom line

This is an exceptional piece of solo engineering — an artist-engineer's life-grade project executed with professional discipline and documented better than most professional code. It earns its **A−** not because the work is merely good, but because it is held to so high a self-imposed standard that the only meaningful criticism left is the gap between how thoroughly it is *built* and how thoroughly the newest parts are *tested* — and even that gap is one of timing, not of care. The fixes in this report would close it; none of them are repairs to a flawed design, and almost all are the engine asking to be held, in one more place, to the rule it already follows everywhere else.
